01 — definition · what makes a step critical
Not all steps are equal.

In a 50-step folding episode, the agent makes 50 dihedral angle decisions. But only a handful of those decisions are critical — moments where the physical state of the protein changes dramatically in a single step.

A critical point is detected when any of these four conditions trigger: an energy drop larger than 1.0 kcal/mol in a single step, the RMSD crossing below the 2Å native-structure threshold, a steric clash that forces a conformational revert, or energy convergence signalling the agent has found a local minimum.

These moments tell the story of folding — where the agent discovered a low-energy conformation, where it hit a barrier, and where it found the native-like structure.

Big Energy Drop
Energy fell more than 1.0 kcal/mol in a single dihedral adjustment. Reward: +8.0. The agent found a significantly more stable conformation.
RMSD Threshold
Cα RMSD vs native structure crossed below 2Å. Reward: +15.0. The agent discovered a near-native protein conformation.
Steric Clash
Two atoms came within 1.5Å — a physically impossible overlap. Reward: −2.0. The move was reverted. The agent learned a barrier.
Convergence
Energy stabilised across 5 consecutive steps (Δ < 0.01 kcal/mol). The agent has found a local energy minimum and stopped improving.
